# Stage 20 - No-Law Boundary Report

## 1. Program Position

Stage 20 consolidates the reinforcement-class probes that opened Phase II of the HAOS-IIP pre-geometry program. Phase I established that the frozen kernel-induced cochain architecture supports structured morphology and localized ordering, but does not produce robust binding or geometry-like persistence under weak local, relational, synchronization, or minimal retarded extensions. Phase II then tested whether bounded substrate back-reaction could supply the missing persistence channel.

The role of Stage 20 is closure rather than exploration. It records the reinforcement-class falsification result in persistence-first terms and defines the resulting architectural boundary. In this sense, Stage 20 is the validated negative plateau that follows Stages 19A-19D.

## 2. Reinforcement Mechanisms Tested

### 2.1 Local Scalar Density Reinforcement (Stage 19A)

Symmetry of feedback:
- local, symmetric, scalar reinforcement driven by smoothed amplitude or occupation density
- support-preserving update of the frozen kernel

Deformation scale:
- bounded and nonzero in non-null runs
- kernel deformation entered the dynamics but remained small, with deviations on the order of `10^-6` to `10^-5`

Observed dynamical effect:
- local kernel response was measurable
- trajectories showed no persistence-relevant reshaping

Persistence outcome:
- no composite lifetime gain
- no binding persistence gain
- no coarse basin persistence gain
- no new stabilized ordering class

### 2.2 Mesoscopic Basin-Residence Reinforcement (Stage 19B / Branch 1)

Symmetry of feedback:
- mesoscopic, symmetric reinforcement driven by coarse basin dwell and occupation-history structure
- bounded residence-memory field projected back onto the frozen kernel support

Deformation scale:
- clearly nonzero and mesoscopic rather than numerically trivial
- reinforcement field norms ranged from roughly `0.2179` to `0.3902`
- kernel deviation norms reached roughly `2.2e-3` to `7.8e-3`

Observed dynamical effect:
- the substrate stored mesoscopic residence history
- no capture, basin locking, or regime reshaping followed

Persistence outcome:
- composite lifetime remained unchanged
- binding persistence remained unchanged
- coarse basin persistence remained unchanged
- no ordering capture or stabilized class appeared

### 2.3 Transport-Corridor Reinforcement (Stage 19B / Branch 2)

Symmetry of feedback:
- mesoscopic, symmetric reinforcement driven by repeated transport-corridor usage
- bounded flow-history field derived from smoothed flux magnitude with finite memory

Deformation scale:
- nonzero but weaker than basin-residence reinforcement
- reinforcement field norms were approximately `4.4e-4` to `5.8e-4`
- kernel deviation norms were approximately `4.4e-6` to `1.17e-5`

Observed dynamical effect:
- transport memory entered the substrate cleanly
- no corridor-selective stabilization, no persistent channel locking, and no regime shift were observed

Persistence outcome:
- no composite lifetime gain
- no binding persistence gain
- no coarse basin persistence gain
- no corridor dwell or locking gain
- no new stabilized ordering class

### 2.4 Directional Flux / Vector Reinforcement (Stage 19C)

Symmetry of feedback:
- antisymmetric or directional mesoscopic reinforcement driven by local flux orientation
- weak vector-like substrate deformation intended to test deflection persistence rather than scalar trapping

Deformation scale:
- bounded and nonzero in all non-null runs
- reinforcement field norms reached roughly `6.86e-5` to `2.27e-4`
- kernel deviation norms reached roughly `1.37e-6` to `4.53e-6`
- max local deformation remained on the order of `10^-5`

Observed dynamical effect:
- directional deflection texture appeared weakly, especially in the corridor control
- no orbital closure, corridor capture, or persistence-relevant ordering stabilization followed

Persistence outcome:
- no composite lifetime gain
- no binding persistence gain
- no coarse basin persistence gain
- no corridor locking gain
- no new stabilized ordering class

### 2.5 Mixed Scalar-Vector Reinforcement (Stage 19D)

Symmetry of feedback:
- combined scalar well shaping and directional flux bias
- symmetric amplitude-memory channel and antisymmetric transport-bias channel applied simultaneously under a strict kernel clamp

Deformation scale:
- the strongest reinforcement response in the tested family
- mixed field norms reached approximately `6.50e-3` to `9.42e-3`
- kernel deviation norms tracked at the same scale
- max local deformation reached approximately `2.83e-2` to `3.47e-2`

Observed dynamical effect:
- both channels were active and the substrate response was clearly mesoscopic
- deflection and deformation increased, but no stable trapping or basin capture followed

Persistence outcome:
- no composite lifetime gain
- no binding persistence gain
- no coarse basin persistence gain
- no corridor dwell or locking gain
- no new stabilized ordering class

## 3. Persistence-Level Result

Across the full weak reinforcement family tested in Stages 19A-19D, the persistence-level observables remained flat against null under the established evaluation ladder.

The consistent result was:
- composite lifetime delta remained null
- binding persistence delta remained null
- basin capture probability remained unchanged
- coarse basin residence remained unchanged
- corridor locking probability remained unchanged
- no new stabilized ordering class was observed
- no base run earned promotion to the `12 -> 24` refinement ladder

This defines a regime-level negative rather than a measurement failure. In every reinforcement branch, the substrate response was active, bounded, and numerically nontrivial. The null result therefore lies at the level of dynamical organization, not instrumentation.

## 4. Structural Interpretation

The tested reinforcement laws show that weak substrate memory can deform the effective kernel without producing persistence-relevant capture.

The dominant structural pattern is:
- weak substrate memory produces deformation without trapping
- propagating packets outrun the induced reinforcement wells before closure can occur
- scalar-like wells induce mild refraction or dispersion shaping rather than composite capture
- vector-like asymmetry induces deflection without orbital closure or transport locking
- mixed reinforcement increases deformation amplitude but still fails to create persistence

The system therefore responds to reinforcement as a transport medium with memory rather than as a medium that self-organizes bound states. Under the tested weak, bounded, reversible back-reaction laws, mesoscopic deformation does not deepen into an attractor that can hold packet structure over time.

The resulting structural conclusion is:

> Weak mesoscopic back-reaction is structurally insufficient for emergent binding in this architecture.

## 5. Boundary Statement

The established Stage 20 boundary is:

> In the tested cochain transverse-packet architecture, all weak local and mesoscopic reinforcement laws tested to date fail to produce geometry-like persistence or bound composite states.

This boundary applies:
- at the tested coupling strengths
- under persistence-first evaluation criteria
- within the current frozen-operator and bounded-back-reaction class
- for the representative morphology set consisting of clustered composite anchors, phase-ordered symmetric triads, and counter-propagating corridors

This is not a statement that persistence is impossible in every extension of the architecture. It is a falsified region of model space: weak reinforcement in local, mesoscopic, directional, and mixed symmetry classes does not supply the missing law.

## 6. Consequences for Model Evolution

Several implications now follow cleanly.

First:
- increasing coupling strength inside the same weak reinforcement family is not automatically justified
- coefficient escalation would weaken the clarity of the boundary result unless a new structural reason exists

Second:
- further scalar-like reinforcement variations are low priority
- local density memory, basin-residence memory, and transport-corridor reinforcement have already constrained the most natural weak scalar and mesoscopic subclasses

Third:
- future exploration should target qualitatively new law classes rather than additional variants of the same reinforcement pattern

The remaining plausible directions now include:
- nonlocal constraint-mediated laws
- topology-changing substrate response
- spinor or exclusion-type dynamics
- effective-law search over operator space
- explicit relational registration dynamics following repeated encounter history

## 7. Program Trajectory After Stage 20

Stage 20 does not terminate the program. It marks a validated negative plateau and provides a clean handoff point.

The next structured options are:
- Stage 21 interaction-registration dynamics, where encounter history becomes an explicit relational state variable
- a positive-path law-search branch over coarse observables, if a bounded effective regulation hypothesis can be defined without retrofitting the failed reinforcement family
- architectural sector expansion, including additional field structure or constraint classes that lie outside the current weak back-reaction framework

The practical meaning of Stage 20 is therefore:
- the reinforcement-class search at weak coupling is closed cleanly
- the program can now move to new law classes without carrying ambiguity about whether weak substrate memory was simply underexplored

## 8. Confirmed Research Boundary

The confirmed boundary after Stages 14-20 is not merely that binding has not yet been observed. The stronger statement is that a broad, physically natural class of weak extensions has now been falsified under persistence-first evaluation.

What is falsified:
- weak local scalar substrate reinforcement as a persistence mechanism
- weak mesoscopic basin-residence reinforcement as a persistence mechanism
- weak transport-corridor reinforcement as a persistence mechanism
- weak directional flux-driven reinforcement as a persistence mechanism
- weak mixed scalar-vector reinforcement as a persistence mechanism

What remains open:
- qualitatively new interaction laws
- new state variables carrying relational registration
- new operator sectors or constraint structures
- coarse effective laws not reducible to reinforcement of the tested substrate class

## Closing Compression

Frozen pre-geometry can organize, but it does not bind.

Phase II extends that boundary:

> Weak substrate-law reinforcement, across local, mesoscopic, directional, and mixed symmetry classes, is insufficient to generate persistence in this architecture at the tested couplings.
