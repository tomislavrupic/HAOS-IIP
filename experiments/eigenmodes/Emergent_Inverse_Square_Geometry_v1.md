# Emergent Inverse-Square Geometry v1

## Purpose

Test whether inverse-distance potential geometry can emerge from the interaction substrate itself, rather than being imposed by hand.

The target chain is:

interaction substrate  
`->` inverse-square geometry  
`->` discrete resonance ladder

The present experiment addresses only the middle step.

## Minimal Substrate Model

Use a spherically symmetric recoverable field `phi(r,tau)` sourced by a localized disturbance `rho(r)` on a continuous 3D substrate.

The local update rule is

$$
\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial \tau}
=
D \left(\phi_{rr} + \frac{2}{r}\phi_r\right) + \rho(r).
$$

This is the minimal radial relaxation law compatible with:

- local propagation
- quadratic substrate response
- 3D radial geometry
- convergence to a stationary recoverable field

The source is a compact Gaussian-like packet near the origin, normalized so that

$$
Q = 4\pi \int_0^\infty r^2 \rho(r)\,dr = 1.
$$

The stationary field then satisfies

$$
-D \left(\phi_{rr} + \frac{2}{r}\phi_r\right) = \rho(r).
$$

## Structural Origin of the Scaling

Outside the source core, `rho(r) = 0`, so the stationary equation becomes

$$
\phi_{rr} + \frac{2}{r}\phi_r = 0.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\frac{1}{r^2}\frac{d}{dr}\left(r^2 \frac{d\phi}{dr}\right)=0.
$$

Integrating once gives

$$
r^2 \phi_r = \text{const}.
$$

So the radial gradient satisfies

$$
|\phi_r| \propto \frac{1}{r^2},
$$

and integrating again yields

$$
\phi(r) = A + \frac{B}{r}.
$$

This is the key result:

- inverse-square force law comes from constant flux through spheres of area `4 pi r^2`
- inverse-distance potential comes from integrating that radial flux law

No inverse-distance structure has to be imposed separately once the source and 3D substrate geometry are fixed.

## Measured Scaling

The measured stationary field fits

$$
\phi(r) \approx A + \frac{B}{r}
$$

with:

- `B = 0.078823`
- `A = -0.002003`
- fit `R^2 = 0.999965`

For unit total source strength and `D = 1`, the expected coefficient is

$$
\frac{Q}{4\pi D} = \frac{1}{4\pi} \approx 0.079577.
$$

The measured relative error is about `0.95%`.

After subtracting the fitted offset `A`, the residual field scales as

$$
\phi(r) - A \propto r^{-0.996009},
$$

which is numerically the expected `1/r`.

The measured radial gradient scales as

$$
|\phi_r| \propto r^{-2.043538},
$$

with fit `R^2 = 0.999877`.

So the simulation gives:

- field `~ 1/r`
- force `~ 1/r^2`

to good accuracy over the clean intermediate radial window.

## Interpretation

This is a positive result.

The inverse-distance geometry does emerge from substrate dynamics, provided the question is asked in the right form:

- not as an instantaneous snapshot of a transient packet
- but as the recoverable stationary field generated by a localized persistent disturbance

That distinction matters. A single free transient packet under quadratic propagation spreads in time and does not itself freeze into a static `1/r` profile. The `1/r` law appears when local propagation is combined with:

- a localized source
- 3D flux conservation
- relaxation into a stationary recoverable field

Under those conditions, inverse-square geometry is not an extra assumption. It is the natural far-field law of the substrate.
